# Realistic Business Simulation Game Design Principles

## Core Mechanics for Realism

1. **Every decision should have an opportunity cost**

   In reality, you can almost never do everything.

   **Example:**

   You have $500,000.

   You could:

   - Open another store.
   - Upgrade your existing factory.
   - Hire better employees.
   - Pay off debt.
   - Save cash for a recession.

   All are good choices.

   Only one can be done.

   The difficulty comes from not knowing which one will be best.

2. **Information should be imperfect**

   Reality never gives perfect numbers.

   Instead of showing

   Demand next month = 14,382 customers

   show

   Market research estimates:

   Demand:

   11,000–16,000 customers

   The player now has uncertainty.

3. **Consequences should be delayed**

   One of the biggest differences between games and reality:

   Bad decisions often feel good at first.

   **Example:**

   Taking a huge loan

   Month 1

   +$3 million

   Feels amazing.

   Month 24

   Interest payments become overwhelming.

   Now the player regrets it.

   Good strategy games punish short-term thinking.

4. **Every advantage creates a new problem**

   Reality works like this.

   You build another factory.

   **Good:**

   More production

   **Bad:**

   More employees  
   More maintenance  
   Higher electricity bills  
   More debt  
   Larger payroll

   Success should create new management problems.

5. **Systems should interact**

   The economy shouldn't have isolated mechanics.

   **Example:**

   Interest rates rise.  
   ↓  
   Businesses borrow less.  
   ↓  
   Construction slows.  
   ↓  
   Steel demand drops.  
   ↓  
   Steel companies lay off workers.  
   ↓  
   Unemployment rises.  
   ↓  
   Consumer spending falls.  
   ↓  
   Restaurants lose customers.  
   ↓  
   Banks receive more loan defaults.  
   ↓  
   Banks tighten lending.  
   ↓  
   Economy slows further.

   One event should ripple through many systems.

6. **Players should never have enough money**

   Cash shortages create interesting decisions.

   Instead of asking:

   "What should I build?"

   Players ask:

   "What can I afford?"

   Those are very different questions.

7. **People should behave irrationally**

   Real people are not optimization algorithms.

   **Consumers**

   - panic buy
   - follow trends
   - overreact
   - trust brands
   - become loyal

   **Businesses**

   - overexpand
   - copy competitors
   - panic during recessions

   **Banks**

   - become too optimistic
   - later become too conservative

   **Governments**

   - make political decisions
   - not always economically optimal

   This makes the simulation much harder to predict.

8. **Luck should matter—but not dominate**

   Reality contains randomness.

   Bad harvest.  
   Pandemic.  
   Bridge collapse.  
   Celebrity endorsement.  
   Factory fire.

   The best players should still outperform weaker players over many games, but they shouldn't be able to predict every event.

9. **Make success create competition**

   Players shouldn't stay on top forever.

   If your grocery chain becomes profitable:

   Competitors notice.

   They:

   - lower prices
   - open nearby
   - advertise heavily
   - hire your employees

   Winning should paint a target on the player's back.

10. **AI should adapt**

    Many games fail because AI follows scripts.

    Instead:

    If player raises prices...  
    AI lowers prices.

    If player dominates exports...  
    AI invests in exports.

    If player monopolizes trucking...  
    Government investigates.

    Competitors cooperate.  
    Banks become cautious.

    Dynamic responses keep the game fresh.

11. **Reputation should matter**

    In reality, reputation takes years to build.

    Lose it once...

    Everyone remembers.

    **Example:**

    Bankruptcy  
    ↓  
    Banks charge higher interest.  
    ↓  
    Suppliers require upfront payment.  
    ↓  
    Employees avoid your company.  
    ↓  
    Customers lose confidence.

    One mistake echoes for years.

12. **Scale should increase complexity**

    Running:

    One store  
    ↓  
    Simple.

    Running:

    300 stores  
    ↓  
    Very difficult.

    Problems appear that never existed before.

    **Examples**

    - logistics
    - fraud
    - regional demand
    - management hierarchy
    - communication
    - quality control

    Large companies should not simply be "small companies with bigger numbers."

13. **The market should evolve**

    Players shouldn't solve the game once.

    Consumer tastes change.  
    Technology improves.  
    Competitors innovate.  
    Government changes laws.  
    Imports become cheaper.  
    Population ages.

    The optimal strategy changes over time.

14. **Don't let math solve everything**

    If spreadsheets always produce the best answer, the game becomes deterministic.

    Introduce factors that resist perfect optimization:

    - changing consumer preferences
    - hidden information
    - competitor reactions
    - politics
    - human error

    Now the player must use judgment, not just calculations.

15. **Failure should come from many small mistakes**

    Real businesses rarely fail because of one catastrophic event.

    Instead:

    Small loan.  
    ↓  
    Another loan.  
    ↓  
    Hire too many people.  
    ↓  
    Demand drops 8%.  
    ↓  
    Inventory piles up.  
    ↓  
    Cash flow weakens.  
    ↓  
    Interest payments increase.  
    ↓  
    Suppliers demand cash.  
    ↓  
    One missed payment.  
    ↓  
    Credit rating falls.  
    ↓  
    Another bad month.  
    ↓  
    Bankruptcy.

    Players should often look back and realize they could have prevented failure several decisions earlier.

16. **The player should have to prioritize**

    A realistic executive never finishes everything.

    At any moment there might be:

    - A labor strike
    - A supplier shortage
    - A product launch
    - A tax audit
    - A competitor price war
    - An acquisition opportunity

    The challenge isn't solving all of them—it's deciding which deserve attention now.

17. **Let the simulation surprise even you**

    The hallmark of a deep strategy game is that the designer cannot predict every outcome.

    For example:

    - A shipping company becomes the richest business because several retailers depend on it.
    - A recession unexpectedly strengthens discount stores.
    - One bank fails, triggering a nationwide credit crunch.
    - A small startup becomes dominant because it enters an overlooked niche.

    When systems interact naturally, interesting stories emerge without being scripted.

18. **Balance realism with playability**

    Real life also contains paperwork, bureaucracy, and repetitive tasks. Those are realistic, but they are rarely fun.

    A good rule is:

    Keep the decisions realistic.  
    Abstract the busywork.

    For example, deciding whether to refinance debt is interesting; manually approving every employee paycheck usually isn't.

## Advanced Simulation Elements

1. **Information is a Commodity**

   Don't let players know everything.

   Instead, create an information economy.

   **Examples:**

   - Market research firms sell demand forecasts.
   - Credit agencies rate businesses.
   - Journalists investigate companies.
   - Competitors spy on each other.
   - Employees leak information.
   - Investors speculate.

   A player with better information has a huge advantage.

2. **Every Person Has a Life**

   Don't make employees numbers.

   Every citizen should have:

   - age
   - education
   - work history
   - personality
   - spending habits
   - savings
   - debt
   - family
   - happiness
   - health
   - skills
   - ambitions

   A worker losing his job doesn't just disappear.

   His family spends less.  
   His children may leave school.  
   His mortgage defaults.  
   His local businesses lose customers.

3. **Trust Becomes a Currency**

   Every relationship has trust.

   **Examples**

   Supplier trusts you  
   ↓  
   Offers lower prices.

   Bank trusts you  
   ↓  
   Lower interest.

   Employees trust you  
   ↓  
   Less turnover.

   Customers trust you  
   ↓  
   Remain loyal during scandals.

   Governments trust you  
   ↓  
   Faster permits.

   Trust takes years to build.  
   One scandal can destroy it.

4. **Logistics Wins Wars**

   Many strategy games ignore logistics.

   Reality doesn't.

   **Imagine:**

   Factory A  
   Produces food.  

   Road floods.  
   Truck cannot travel.  

   Stores run out.  
   Customers panic.  
   Competitors profit.

   One bridge can change an economy.

5. **Businesses Have Internal Politics**

   Large companies shouldn't act like one brain.

   Departments compete.  
   Managers hide mistakes.  
   Executives disagree.  
   Regional offices pursue different goals.

   This creates realistic inefficiency.

6. **Human Psychology**

   People rarely act rationally.

   **Examples**

   **Consumers**

   - fear missing out
   - panic buying
   - brand loyalty
   - revenge boycotts

   **Investors**

   - greed
   - fear
   - bubbles

   **Employees**

   - jealousy
   - burnout
   - ambition

   **Competitors**

   - ego
   - pride
   - revenge

   Psychology should move markets.

7. **Black Swan Events**

   Rare events.

   Not random disasters every year.

   **Examples**

   - volcanic eruption
   - cyber attack
   - oil spill
   - pandemic
   - war
   - new technology
   - revolutionary invention

   These completely change optimal strategies.

8. **Political Pressure**

   Government shouldn't be one button.

   Instead

   Businesses lobby.  
   Citizens protest.  
   Media campaigns influence elections.  
   Labor unions negotiate.  
   Environmental groups sue.  

   Governments respond.

   Politics should emerge naturally.

9. **Hidden Weaknesses**

   A company can appear healthy.

   **Reality:**

   - Huge hidden debt.
   - Poor maintenance.
   - Fraud.
   - Corruption.
   - Outdated equipment.

   Eventually something breaks.

10. **Culture**

    Every city should have culture.

    **Example**

    Industrial city  
    Likes practical products.

    Tourist city  
    Luxury goods.

    College town  
    Technology.

    Agricultural region  
    Pickup trucks.

    Marketing changes by location.

11. **Reputation Has Memory**

    People don't instantly forgive.

    **Example**

    Food poisoning incident.  

    Even after fixing it:  

    Customers remain skeptical.  
    Banks worry.  
    Investors hesitate.  
    Competitors advertise against you.

    Recovery takes years.

12. **Supply Chains**

    Instead of:  

    Farm → Store  

    Think  

    Farm  
    ↓  
    Processor  
    ↓  
    Distributor  
    ↓  
    Warehouse  
    ↓  
    Transport company  
    ↓  
    Retailer  
    ↓  
    Customer

    Every link can fail.

13. **Resource Scarcity**

    Nothing should be infinite.

    **Examples**

    - skilled workers
    - truck drivers
    - engineers
    - fertilizer
    - fuel
    - electricity
    - land
    - water

    Scarcity creates meaningful competition.

14. **Time is a Resource**

    Executives cannot solve everything.

    Maybe each month the player gets

    100 management points.

    Every decision costs points.

    Visit a factory? 10  
    Meet investors? 15  
    Inspect stores? 20  
    Negotiate loans? 25

    Now prioritization becomes a game mechanic.

15. **Businesses Learn**

    AI shouldn't simply react.

    It should remember.

    **Example**

    Player constantly undercuts prices.  

    Competitors stop competing on price.  

    Instead  
    They improve quality.  
    Or focus on premium customers.

    The market evolves.

16. **Innovation Isn't Guaranteed**

    Spending on research shouldn't guarantee success.

    Maybe

    $10 million spent.  

    20% chance Nothing useful.  
    5% Revolutionary breakthrough.  
    75% Small improvement.

    This mirrors reality.

17. **Employees Aren't Interchangeable**

    Every employee has:

    - creativity
    - reliability
    - honesty
    - leadership
    - learning speed
    - adaptability
    - stress tolerance

    Replacing one manager might reduce company performance for years.

18. **Crime Exists**

    Not just disasters.

    **Examples**

    - Employee theft.
    - Corporate espionage.
    - Counterfeit goods.
    - Tax fraud.
    - Bribery.
    - Money laundering.
    - Smuggling.

    The player decides how much to invest in prevention versus accepting some losses.

19. **Markets Have Memory**

    Consumers remember.  
    Investors remember.  
    Banks remember.  
    Governments remember.

    If your company defaults once,  

    Ten years later  

    Banks may still charge higher interest.

20. **Businesses Age**

    Companies shouldn't stay modern forever.

    Equipment becomes obsolete.  
    Buildings deteriorate.  
    Software ages.  
    Processes become inefficient.

    Players must continuously reinvest.

21. **Dynamic Consumer Trends**

    Demand shifts organically.

    **Examples:**

    - Health-conscious consumers reduce sugary drinks.
    - Electric vehicles become fashionable.
    - Local products gain popularity after an import disruption.
    - A celebrity endorsement causes a sudden spike in demand.

    These trends shouldn't be scripted—they should emerge from events, marketing, demographics, and social influence.

22. **Interconnected Financial System**

    Banks shouldn't operate independently.

    Banks lend to:  

    - Businesses
    - Households
    - Other banks
    - Governments

    If one major bank fails:  

    Credit freezes.  
    Businesses can't refinance loans.  
    Construction projects halt.  
    Layoffs increase.  
    Consumer spending falls.  
    More defaults occur.

    This creates the possibility of systemic financial crises rather than isolated bankruptcies.

23. **AI With Different Personalities**

    Every AI-controlled business owner should think differently.

    **Examples:**

    - Risk Taker: Expands aggressively with heavy borrowing.
    - Conservative: Maintains large cash reserves and avoids debt.
    - Innovator: Invests heavily in research.
    - Cost Cutter: Focuses relentlessly on efficiency.
    - Brand Builder: Spends heavily on marketing and customer loyalty.
    - Predator: Accepts short-term losses to eliminate competitors.

    This means the same market can evolve very differently from one game to the next.

24. **Multiple Paths to Success**

    Avoid a single "best" strategy.

    A player should be able to become wealthy through:

    - Manufacturing
    - Retail
    - Agriculture
    - Finance
    - Logistics
    - Technology
    - Real estate
    - Energy
    - Entertainment
    - Investing
    - Venture capital
    - Franchising

    Each path should require different skills and respond differently to economic conditions.

25. **Emergent Stories**

    The greatest strategy games create stories that weren't scripted.

    Imagine hearing players say:

    > "I started as a fisherman, bought refrigerated trucks, accidentally monopolized seafood distribution, caused two competitors to go bankrupt, triggered layoffs across the island, a bank suffered major loan losses, the government investigated me for antitrust violations, and I had to sell part of my company to survive."

    Or:

    > "A recession wiped out luxury retailers, but because I owned discount supermarkets and warehouses with large cash reserves, I expanded while everyone else was selling assets."

    Those stories don't come from quests or scripted events—they emerge from interacting systems.
